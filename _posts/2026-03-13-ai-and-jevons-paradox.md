AI is everywhere. There is basically no person I know except maybe my grandparents who doesn't use AI at least once a week, if not multiple times a day.  
Even though ChatGPT was released on November 30, 2022, most people can't imagine a world without it anymore.

We use it for researching recipes, writing messages, drawing pictures, or whatever task we currently have to deal with.  
Even if you don't use a classic chatbot like ChatGPT, you are probably using AI without even noticing it, integrated into the operating systems or software we use day to day, like Apple Intelligence in macOS or Microsoft Copilot in the Office 365 software.

This begs the question: If essentially all tasks we address are handled with AI in some way or another, what does this do to us?

## Jevons Paradox

One step back. There are multiple instances in human history where we invented new technologies that automated parts of our lives or made them more efficient.  
AI is only one revolution of many. It is not the first time people have been afraid of inevitable progress.

An often cited concept regarding this topic is the Jevons Paradox.  
In a nutshell, Jevons Paradox describes the correlation between rising efficiency and the number of employees needed in the affected sector.

Naively, one would think that a more efficient machine would result in needing fewer people. The exact opposite is often the case.  
This is caused by the rebound effect. If a product gets cheaper due to efficiency while maintaining every other aspect, demand will rise, and likewise the number of people needed to meet that demand.

## Is AI the new steam engine?

I know this question might be a bit cheesy, but I think it is not far from reality.  
Like the steam engine, AI transforms many aspects of our lives. It speeds up many processes and lets our productivity rise.

But in my opinion, there is one key difference:  
The steam engine did things we already did, just faster.  
AI does things we do, but faster and less creatively.

Every time I use AI to solve a task, like coding for my job or planning a travel route, it often feels generic.  
The solution might be quick and somewhat reliable (we will come to this part later), but it won't be groundbreaking or particularly interesting.

This results in a weird situation where everyone using AI gets generic solutions, and everyone who doesn't is much slower and loses the race.

## The computer is always right

AI in combination with our human psychology has another major flaw.  
We collectively learned in the last few decades that the computer does not make mistakes. This is called automation bias.

This might be true for a calculator or your Excel sheet, but not for AI.  
Since AI as we know it in 2026 is quite new, our perception of the reliability of computers has not yet adapted.

AI's rise has led to a flood of misinformation in (social) media and therefore in our brains.

To be sure that the information the AI gives us is correct, we would have to fact-check every detail. But who proves to us that the media we use to fact-check is not the result of a hallucinating AI in the first place?

## Solution

To end on a good note: I think AI can be used to vastly improve our way of working, learning, and living.  
In my opinion, we simply have to adjust how we use AI in our workflows.

Maybe this article will age very poorly, but I think letting multiple AI agents iterate over themselves will not be the future.

We should be more critical about using AI as a search engine replacement and never trust generated output unconditionally.

A current example in software engineering for such behavior is vibe coding.  
In my opinion, vibe coding is dangerous and leads to code that is not very maintainable. Developers may slowly lose the ability to fully understand their code because they code less and "vibe code" more.

I also use AI, even if I might sound rather critical in this post. But I use it for smaller tasks where I can quickly check the output or when I just need a starting point for a task. For me, it acts more like a preprocessor for my work.

This lets me explore possible solutions without directly giving me the full implementation.
